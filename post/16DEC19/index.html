My First Semester in College

December 16th, 2019

I finally did it. I managed to go to college, a university, in fact. Something that if you told me I would do a year ago, I’d have laughed in your face. I’m proud to say that I’m currently working on my Computer Science degree… next semester. I was initially a Computer Engineering major. Apart from general requirement classes, the only class I had relating to my degree was an Electrical Engineering course. Th­ings were all fine and dandy for the first month or so, but steadily things turned bad.

My first red flag was my professor. He opened saying that about 50% of everyone in the class actually passes, and that’s including transfer students. Alright, I’ve dealt with worse odds, my current job’s training had a 33% pass-rate with people who retried. Then everyone started dropping like flies. Essentially, if you don’t show up to class, you won’t pass. Which is fair, if someone’s not going to even show up then they really shouldn’t be an engineer. But two thirds of the time, it wasn’t even worth showing up to class. The class was held three times a week. We were taught once a week. The other two classes? Our instructor would rant about goal setting, motivation, and sometimes bring in guest speakers to show us some graphic art they made on a raspberry pi. Which is fine, and admittedly cool, but this is the foundation of our careers. Shouldn’t we be doing more example problems? Shouldn’t we make sure we’re actually getting this right? Apparently not.

The majority of the repetition (that myself and everyone else needed) came from the homework. Alright, so we apply what we learn on the one day that we actually receive instruction on the homework, and thus drill it into our heads, right? Sorta. The homework took hours to complete, one time it required, not even exaggerating, THIRTY sheets of paper. More assignments only required about three to four, admittedly. So why did that take us hours? My professor had a thing for puzzles. Essentially, every problem would have a trick to it, one that normally required you to read between the lines to crack. We weren’t learning formulas. We weren’t learning problem solving. We were solving puzzles. In the foundational class of the degree. Amazing.

Surely he must notice that so many people are failing, you ask. And yes, he did. He hosted a study program once a month to help those who struggle. Great! So it’s not so impossible! I was just overreacting! Well, like all things with this class, it had a catch too. You had to know precisely what to ask. I couldn’t just go in and say, “Hey, could you walk me through this problem? I’m having some trouble seeing where I’m going wrong here.”. Why not? Because then he’d ask where your notes were when we did it in class. Weird, but alright. Then he’d point to the notes and say your answer is in there. Why the hell did I even come here then? I ended up just trying to teach myself the entire semester with tutorials online and Khanacademy, except then I found out that he teaches the subject in an entirely unique way, a way that you must use on the tests and homeworks or else it’s wrong, even if you got the right answer, and even if that’s the way actual engineers do things. Another cute thing about the class is that none of the grades are posted. You’re expected to track them all yourself, which is fine and dandy, but miss one class and all of a sudden you’re uncertain if the grade you’re tracking is even accurate.

That class made me realize something important. I can’t rely on my instructors to learn. I essentially have to teach myself the entire subject matter prior to even walking in the first day. I did great in all my other classes, my other instructors actually did teach me. But now I have a fear that, if he’s still allowed to teach, despite having the majority of his class fail every semester, how many others like him are there? So I switched my major to Computer Science, something I already know and have been doing for years. Even if I get the worst professor in the world next semester, it won’t matter a bit. I already have all the skills. I even managed to solve the final project of the class I’ll be taking next semester. In an hour. While the class was given two weeks.

So what about those who did pass the class? They all had prior knowledge. One guy literally browsed Reddit the entire semester because he already knew everything. My lab partner, one of the better-off students, did robotics and was an honor student his whole life. The majority of the rest of the class who made it had similar stories. Which further confirms my theory: the class didn’t teach anyone. Either you pass already knowing everything, or you fail. There were some exceptions, students who formed a pack together and shared answers and formed study groups, but even some of them weren’t safe. So I suppose now it’s my turn. My turn to be the guy who browses Reddit the entire class while everyone else fails. It’s a terrible system, but I guess that’s just the one we live in.
