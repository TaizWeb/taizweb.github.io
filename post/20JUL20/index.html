"Meritocracy in Theory and Practice" - A Response

July 20th, 2020

I haven't been posting blog posts in a while due to my current obligations, but this 'article' stuck out to me to such a degree that I felt the need to correct the many fallacies contained in the 'article'. I suggest you read Meritocracy in Theory and Practice by Coraline Ada before reading further.

This may read a bit odd, as I repeatedly alternated between referring to you, the reader, and the hypothetical Coraline reading this. However I feel as if it enhances the reading experience, so I kept it in.

Claim #1

Our lovely Coraline states the following: "Because of the traditional organization of Western society, white men have considerably more free time than white women do...". This is a straight up lie. Taken from The US Bureau of Labor Statistics, men work an average of 52 minutes longer than women per day. So in actuality, women have an extra five hours a week (assuming a five day work week). So much for "considerably more free time than women do", huh Coraline?

Claim #2

Here Coraline attempts to explain the disparity between women vs men in the field of software development. As stated: "Women make up about 30% of all software developers [...] the most likely causes [of this disparity] are social, political, and economical". Tell me something Coraline, why is it that so few men decide to go into health related professions? According to The National Center for Education Statistics(figure 3), only 16% of men pursue this field. I can explain this disparity, it's "social, political, and economical" reasons, right? Men must be discriminated against. Is that correct, my dear Coraline? That's wrong, you say, men simply don't want to pursue this field. And alas, you've answered your own question.

As a side note, before you try to pull some "it's learned discrimination" garbage, you should note that figure 3 also says that women make up 60% of business majors. A field in which I'm certain you were assuming was dominated by men.

Claim #3

Coraline's next point is a bit of a word salad, so I'll dumb it down to her complaint that open source software mirrors corporate software in the sense that "These projects tend to be governed either by a single charismatic individual (the benevolent dictator model) or a small cadre of individuals". What's so wrong about that? In nearly every single successful project/nation/organization, there's someone (or multiple someones) on top. And yes, this happens to be a "meritocracy", and yes, the people leading are the most talented. But there's a reason for this. If a team of carpenters was building you a new house, you'd want the guy who's built the most houses in charge. Hell, let's do away with merit entirely, as you seem to desire. You get a rag tag team of carpenters, none of which have actually built a house before (besides the one guy who built a shed once). Would you rather, my dear Coraline, have them in charge of assembling your house, or the ones led by the guy who knows what he's doing? Careful, your roof will fall in on you if you choose incorrectly.

Going back to the software side of things, I wager that Linux would have never been as successful as it is today if it lacked the leadership Torvalds provided. Was he cruel to contributors if they lacked merit? Yes, in fact he's known for his cruelty. But if you had a guy trying to take down the foundation holding up the house you're building, you'd yell at him too. You'll be thankful when your house doesn't collapse, or when your operating system doesn't brick itself.

Claim #4

"The ruling class are gatekeepers: they are the arbiters of the ill-defined concept of merit, they set the tone for participation in the community, and they establish and normalize standards of behavior that are acceptable in a given community."

Yeah, I wouldn't want the guy who wandered over to the house I'm trying to build, who's never held a hammer before in his life, working on the house.

Claim #5

"...intelligence above all other traits is a form of erasure. Intelligence is notoriously hard to measure-- ask any psychometrician."

What does intelligence have to do with anything? Since you're implying it's to be measured, I'm assuming you're referring to IQ here. Which makes me very interested as to what project asked you to take an IQ test before making a pull request. You then state that because of this difficulty in measuring IQ, that maintainers instead make the call themselves. According to her, the thought process of said maintainers goes like this:

"“I believe you are intelligent or capable or a talented coder because you believe the same things that I believe, and you meet the standards that I hold myself to.”".

Really? You're telling me that just based on a snippet of code from a commit, maintainers can infer their beliefs? Or perhaps you imply that maintainers perform a background check on the authors of every single pull request to first make sure they have "the same beliefs"? Likely story Coraline.

Claim #6

I'm not even going to quote this one. Essentially some tech companies (note that the one in question wasn't even an open source project) made facial recognition software that had a hard time identifying people who weren't white. I have a question of my own, why do game developers target Windows over Linux? Oh, because the majority of their potential buyers use Windows. And in a nation where the majority of the population is white...

Though I do agree that this is bad practice, and that before releasing their facial recognition software, they should cover all the major use-cases. Not just the majority, in the same way I believe that games should release Windows and Linux ports at the same time, despite it taking up more development time.

Claim #7

The article starts to lose cohesion at this point. Coraline compares meritocracy to slavery, as if the state of the way open source projects are handled can even remotely compare to the suffering caused by slavery. Then she uses an incredibly poorly adapted metaphor to describe some sort of monster that basically ruins software development, ignoring how successful (and important) going by merit is to a project. By the way, the single metaphor is 800+ words, so I wouldn't blame you if you glossed over it. I'll summarize.

Essentially you have 10 developers, which each get 10 units of "resource" (which would be time, in this case). These 10 developers can produce 2x output of the resource they're given, but later an 11th one is added and he can produce 3x. So they give the 3x developer more resources, taken from the not-so-productive developers, as it would result in a higher output. Coraline argues that this leads to exclusion. This metaphor, being as poorly adapted as it is, has a fatal flaw. The "resource" isn't tangible in software development. It's not like developers get some magic code juice to write more code. Recall what the resource really is: Time. And time cannot be given freely between people, especially not software developers. So rather than the equation being that two developers are kicked out for their time, resulting in (8 * 2) + (1 * 3) = 19, it comes out to (10 * 2) + (1 * 3) = 22. How is this a bad thing, Coraline?

My Final Thoughts

Meritocracy leads to projects being successful. Without it, there would be no leaders in projects. They would be no direction. And there would be no software. Does this result in gatekeeping? Yes. But this gatekeeping exists for a very good reason. I'm not well versed in operating system development. But if Linux followed your "ideals" of abolishing meritocracy, then I'd be able to make a pull request just fine, and have my unsafe, poorly written, and potentially harmful, code merged into Linux. And in a world that relies more and more on technology, this can be life-threatening. Imagine mainframes keeping hospitals running update their kernel and end up going down, because "meritocracy bad" and everyone should be able to contribute, even if, they quite frankly, have no business in writing code for an operating system.

Let's go a step further, let's ban it from society itself. You're in critical condition and you need surgery. But you want a doctor... with a degree? That's meritocracy! You should be ashamed! Don't you know most doctors are white men? Nobody else is getting a chance! Here, take this man off the street and hand him a scalpel. He wants to be a doctor, so let him do your surgery. After all, it should be accessible to everyone, right? Not just those evil evil privileged men. Nonono, you stutter, there's a reason why people who want to be doctors require qualifications and skills. And that's exactly the way it should be. A meritocracy. Thanks for reading.
