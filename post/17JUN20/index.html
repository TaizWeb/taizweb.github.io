Project Proton Released!

June 17th, 2020

Seeing as how I managed to get something done in a weekend for Bitter Jam, I wanted to see what I could accomplish with a month. And that was Project Proton!

Jam Start

The first thing I did when the jam started was plan out "milestones" of things that needed to be done, and the days to do them. This included everything from the code, graphics, sound, and writing. I had a day at the end to playtest and polish, as I didn't want to push something that had game-breaking bugs in it. I'm once again using my own engine, Heartbeat, for the jam to make sure it has enough features to make a full game.

Planning Phase

I originally toyed with the idea of just making a demo for Project Neutron, but I quickly realized that it would go far beyond the scope of the month, having to create entire equipment menus, figure out how to get multiple weapons going, and also draw some portrait art. As I don't have a scanner to hand-draw the portraits nor do I have a drawing tablet (or hell, and experience with drawing aside from pixel art), I ended up deciding to create an entirely different game altogether. I eventually decided to create a game more akin to Metroid than Castlevania, as I knew it'd be far easier implementing a simple arm blaster than it would programming in swinging weapons.

Story

The thing I wanted most in Project Proton was a captivating story. I was looking at past jam entries for Metroidvania month and a number of them would just tack it on in the description, if they decided to add one at all. This isn't really a fault of them, as I can't honestly name a Metroidvania with a plot as a focus aside from some of the later Castlevania games. I actually had no idea what the plot would even be during the first week, as I was really working on the story for Project Neutron and had no clue how I could integrate it with Proton. Since Proton was going to fire projectiles, I figured it'd have to be a futuristic game, so I set it's story a hundred or so years ahead of Neutron's.

I had another problem: How could I convey the story in a Metroidvania? The game was going to be rather short, so I'd have had to have a character talking to Proton every 5 minutes to get it across. My solution? Terminals. I scattered them across the game, with each giving a tidbit of lore explaining why the lab was in the state it was, and some background on what the lab was even for. With this set, I began on the graphics.

Art

I am not an artist. I may be trying to be, but I consider myself a programmer first and foremost. I didn't want to try to get an actual pixel artist on board, as I disliked the idea of having them do charity work for my game. So I installed Aseprite and went to work. A lot of the art suffered to my inexperience, but I feel like as the jam progressed, my own skills did as well. Some of my later sprites were fairly good, and I was even complimented on some of my animations as I experimented with lighting. The hardest part was creating a walking animation, as I had to animate all the limbs and time it right for it to be believable. For the most part, I feel like Proton's walk cycle turned out okay, aside from his arms. This was the reason I had Proton leave the room with Specks, since I really didn't want to have to create another walking animation just for him.

Music

I actually had a guy all lined up to compose some tracks for me, but later into the jam it became apparent that his schedule was too busy to do it. By a stroke of sheer luck, I found someone else who was interested, despite only having three days to compose everything. I have no complaints with his work, and I'm very grateful that he managed to also create the sound effects for the game as well. Had I not met him, I would've likely tried to compose myself, or in a worst case scenario, left the game without music.

Caveats

I ended up losing about five days of the jam due to my job. I couldn't take my computer with me so I had nothing done by the end of it. And the make matters worse, I felt incredibly burnt out after I got back, with only a week left to complete the game. I had to cut a lot of the content in the game. Originally I planned a grapple upgrade, a charge beam, and a tribeam for Proton to eventually wield. But due to the time constraints, I cut them out altogether. I had to cut a boss out too, one that would have charged at the player, with the player then having to shoot it from behind to damage it. At less than two days left, I nearly cut the final boss from the game as well. However, I felt it an important part of the game, and could give it that proper climax that most Metroidvanias end with. I stayed up all night programming him in, and eventually managed to complete him with just a day left.

Out of Time

I wanted a beta out to share with my friends, so they could give it a play-test so I could get rid of any bugs for release. Due to the bugs I'd found myself with the ending cutscene, I had no choice but to release it as is, with some game-breaking bugs to ship.

Kinda a Beta

Some of my close friends played the game, and had to restart multiple times due to falling out of the world because of buggy door code, or they'd turn invisible in the final sequence due to the way I handled the Zero cutscene animations. I got roasted a little for those, but the jam host informed me that there was still another day reserved for people with issues submitting. Thankfully, this meant I could release a quick patch to fix some of the bugs. I managed to fix all the game-breaking ones, and fix a bug where the player could be attacked while reading terminals. I did a play-test, and the game seemed nearly flawless aside from some minor bugs with collisions. I then watched a streamer play my game to completion, and he was quite happy with how it all turned out. Some of my other friends played it too, and had no problems that the first build had. I suppose I got my beta after all.

Looking Back

One of my top complaints was the lack of any sort of any sort of save-points. Meaning that if you died, the game would completely restart. Most people didn't have the issue, as the game is fairly easy to beat, but for those who didn't play much videogames, this was a huge annoyance. I feel like if I just managed my time better, or planned save points from day one, I could have pulled it off. And having extra time to test my game would have been great, as two of my (currently) three reviews were playing the unpatched version, so that might have tanked my score a bit.

Final Thoughts

In all, I think my game was great. I said what I wanted to say, and did what I wanted to do. Everyone who's played the game said they liked the story, and in just a month I managed to ship a complete 20 minute experience. As for now, I'm taking a small hiatus to work on my Heartbeat engine and writing documentation for it, although I'm open to doing more jam games in the future. I would like to start on Project Neutron, as that'll be my biggest game (and project) yet. I'll likely start posting regular updates on things I add to the game, and perhaps release a demo sometime. Thanks for reading.
