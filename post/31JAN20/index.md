# Layman's Guide to the Fediverse

Building on my last post [The Problem with Centralization](https://taizweb.github.io/post/15JAN20/index.html), I decided to write a quick guide to the fediverse, one for the average user.

You see, a lot of people looking for an alternative to Twitter will often stumble upon a little something called Mastodon, but are then quickly put off by a plethera of complex words, such as "instance", "federation", "decentralization", and three different "timelines" all running concurently. A lot of people on Twitter poke fun at it for being overly complex and requiring technical knowledge. Well, **it doesn't.**

### How do I get started with this, "Fediverse" thing then?
Easy. Go sign up for [shitposter.club](https://shitposter.club) (SPC) and have fun.

### Why that one?
Because it has a fair amount of users, no restrictions, and I believe it to be a good instance to start out on.

### What do you mean, start out on?
As someone who's been on the fediverse for over three years, I could name a dozen servers off the top of my head that I'd happily move to. But you, as an outsider, can't. So go make an account on SPC, have some fun, and then if you don't like _that_ server, you'll know of quite a few others you can move to after.

### Are there apps?
Yes! I personally reccomend Tusky for android and Amaroq for iOS. Although there's dozens to choose from if you happen to not like them.

### It seems like you're babying me here.
I am. Because I don't want to make the same mistake as other guides and make it seem like you need to be a "computer person" to be able to use it. But if you're particularly daring, I'll explain more below.

~~~

## Instances
To start off, I lied earlier. They aren't called "servers" but rather "instances". I chose server instead since nearly everyone knows what those art, but make no mistake: instances _are_ servers. They're called instances because they run an instance, or copy, of fediverse software. There's two commonly used softwares for the fediverse, those being [Mastodon](https://github.com/tootsuite/mastodon) and [Pleroma](https://git.pleroma.social/pleroma/pleroma). A server running those then hosts a mini Twitter-clone.

## Federation
So, the Fediverse is called such since it's a **fed**erated un**iverse** of instances. Basically, instances that have the software running on them are able to communicate with each other thanks to using a common set of rules defined in the software. So while you may be on SPC, I could be on another instance and we could still follow each other and send bad jokes between us. This is known as **decentralization**. Twitter is centralized because it all runs under one roof, so to speak. If Twitter were to ban you, you'd have nowhere else to go, and will thus lose your friends. Thanks to decentralization, getting banned from one instance means you can simply make a new account somewhere else and still talk to your friends. A digestable example of this I'm certain you know of is email. Like how you can be on Gmail and still talk to Hotmail, despite being different services.

## Mastodon and Pleroma
Building on this concept of two unlike services talking to each other, Pleroma instances and Mastodon instances can seamlessly talk to each other. It doesn't matter which one the server you choose runs.

## Timelines
Alright, so you get access to three timelines out of box. The first is the **Home Timeline**. This is where the posts of the people you follow will appear. This is the exact same as the Twitter feed.
The second is the **Local Timeline**. This is where you can see the posts of everyone on your instance, for example, everyone on SPC. This is a good way to find new people to follow and make new friends, as well as to have some content to look at.
The final one is **The Whole Known Network**. This is where every post from every instance shows up. It moves very fast and often has non-english posts in it. I don't reccomend trying to use this to follow people from here. Rather, I suggest folllowing people on your instance, then following who they boost (retweet, in terms of Twitter) and building your followings that way.

### Well this is all neat and dandy, but why should I bother if I'm already established on Twitter?
Allow me to sell you on it.
* 500 character limit, most instances increase this even further
* Custom emojis
* Support for **bold** and _italics_
* You can finally have the @ handle of your dreams
* No fear of getting banned by an algorithm
* Being able to hide stuff with a content warning, useful for spoilers
* An #nsfw tag, so you can safely browse in public
* No ads
* No shadowbanning
* No creepy algorithm showing you what it thinks you'll like
* Multiple apps and themes to use
