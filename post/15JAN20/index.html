The Problem With Centralization

January 15th, 2020

So I got banned from Twitter. And I'm still trying to process how exactly I feel about it. On one hand, it's a huge portion of my life, just gone. My highschool years of me growing up, the little ramblings I'd post that would eventually lead to me becoming the person (and programmer) I am today, all gone. The relationships I forged, the friends I made, the shitty little memes I made, never to be seen again. Luckily, I predicted this. A month prior I requested my Twitter archive and I've been looking through it when I'd feel nostalgic. It's truly disappointing that the service I was so fond of just decided to kick me out one day, with no reason given aside from "platform manipulation and spam", which is a bit ironic since I only posted a dozen times the past year and had my account privated. But alas, what can you do with centralization?

Now for the other side to this: I'd already moved on. This isn't the first time Twitter's gone on a ban-spree. In fact, they seem to happen fairly regularly whenever they tweak their algorithm to dictate what accounts are bad and which aren't. Since early 2017, I've been living on a little something called the fediverse. The main appeal of which being the decentralized nature of the platform. No single person owns it, and there's hundreds of servers to choose from. The admin of the server you live on decides they don't like you anymore and bans you? No worries. There's over a thousand other alternatives. Hell, you can even go off and host your own if you so please. In Twitter's early days it advertised itself as a platform where everyone had a voice, you could tweet at Shaq and he just might respond to you, something groundbreaking at the time. It was a cool concept that attracted everyone from celebrities to your average joe.

However, Twitter has went back on that. Now there's shadowbanning, where not even the people who explicitly follow you can see your posts. There's ads everywhere. Certain people get their replies automatically hidden by default to anyone they reply to. People can literally hide replies they don't like away. So much for free speech, huh? I saw a post the other day about a Government site getting hacked, placing the blame on Iran. A security analyst quickly replied and stated that the hacking could have been done by anyone, and that there's no concrete evidence of Iran involvement. What happened? The author of the tweet hid his replies. Then numerous other accounts began to retweet the post, some made their own posts based on it, and in general misinformation quickly spread. The kicker? It was traced back to a Finnish hacking group on Telegram. Stunning job.

Putting all that aside, I truly believe decentralization to be the future. The fediverse has millions of accounts already and it booming in popularity. Since the GitHub buyout, people are beginning to move more towards their own git servers. Bitcoin is getting more accessible, so people don't have to rely on Patreon and gamble that they won't get banned for the content they produce. Peertube, a YouTube alternative, has had it's fair share of success. These tech giants saw they had a monopoly and began to abuse their users. After all, they have no where else to go... right? I hope that one day everything will be decentralized, and we won't have to worry about if some algorithm change results in our livelihood being terminated. That one day, everyone can have an equal say and produce the content they want without fear. That one day, we will win back the free and open internet.

EDIT: This mostly turned into a post on why Twitter is bad, however these same concepts can be applied to just about any centralized service out there. I plan to make another post in the future that'll (hopefully) be less of a Twitter rant.
